# World — Worm (Parahumans)

Campaign world reference. Setting based on *Worm* by Wildbow. Load-time core — kept small. Full quest/location bank lives in `world-nodes.md`; full cast in `npcs-full.md`; the canonical storyline spine in `arc.md`.

## World Foundations

- **Setting:** Earth Bet, ~2011. A world identical to ours until **1982**, when the first parahuman ("Scion") appeared. Three decades of superpowers have left the world poorer, more fractured, and more dangerous than our own. Whole regions are abandoned. Society is visibly fraying under the weight of capes.
- **Primary city:** **Brockton Bay**, a declining port city in the northeast US. Once a shipping hub; now economically gutted after its ferry closed and the docks died. High unemployment, gang-controlled districts, a small-but-dense cape population. This is the campaign's home turf for the early arcs.
- **Tone:** Dark, grounded, escalating. Powers are dramatic but consequences are real; victories cost something. Moral lines blur — "heroes" and "villains" are labels more than truths. Bleak but not hopeless; human connection and clever resolve matter as much as power.
- **Powers ("the wormverse" frame):** Every parahuman has a **trigger event** — a moment of profound trauma, stress, or loss that grants power. Powers are intimately shaped by the trigger and the person. They are not infinitely flexible: each cape has a *thing they do*, with surprising depth but real limits. (Mechanically mapped to D&D 5e: see "Power → 5e mapping" below.)
- **Cape culture:** An informal set of social rules ("unwritten rules") keeps cape conflict survivable — don't unmask people in their civilian lives, don't go after families, the worst threats trigger temporary truces. The PRT/Protectorate maintain a public hero institution; villains operate in the open as long as they don't cross certain lines.

## The Classification System

The PRT classifies powers by threat-response, not by what they "are." A cape often has several tags. Use these as shorthand when introducing capes and when the player reasons about a fight:

| Class | What it flags |
|---|---|
| **Brute** | Enhanced durability/strength; hard to hurt |
| **Mover** | Mobility — speed, flight, teleport, travel |
| **Shaker** | Area effects — affects a zone, not a target |
| **Blaster** | Ranged offensive attacks |
| **Striker** | Touch/melee-range power effect |
| **Thinker** | Information, senses, analysis, precognition |
| **Master** | Controls minions/creatures or affects minds |
| **Tinker** | Builds technology beyond current science |
| **Stranger** | Stealth, infiltration, perception-dodging |
| **Changer** | Alters own form |
| **Trump** | Affects powers themselves |
| **Breaker** | Shifts into an altered state to use other powers |

Threat is rated by number (e.g. "Brute 5"). Endbringers and Scion are off the scale.

## Three Truths

- **One settlement — Brockton Bay.** A dying port city carved up between gangs and watched over by an understaffed Protectorate. The Boardwalk and Docks, the Trainyard, Downtown, the abandoned ferry. Everyone here is one bad month from leaving or breaking.
- **One threat — the slow collapse.** Locally: the three-way gang war (ABB, Empire 88, Merchants) tightening its grip. Globally and inevitably: the **Endbringers** — near-unkillable monsters that surface every few months to end cities — and the secret rot beneath the whole world that the late arcs uncover. The threat is not a villain to punch; it is the world running out of time.
- **One mystery — where powers come from, and why.** Why trigger events? Why does Scion drift through the world doing token good and nothing more? What connects every cape? The campaign's spine is the gradual, devastating answer.

## Threat Escalation Arc

Maps the 30 canonical arcs onto the skill's 5-stage threat model. Start at **Stage 1**.

1. **Now — Street level (Arcs 1–6):** Gang war in Brockton Bay. The protagonist's debut, falling in with the Undersiders, the first real cape fights (Lung, the bank job, the ABB's bombs). Stakes: turf, reputation, survival.
2. **Rising — The city in play (Arcs 7–10):** Bakuda's bombing campaign, Leviathan's attack on Brockton Bay, the truce and its aftermath. The city is permanently changed. Stakes: the city itself.
3. **Turn — Power and consequence (Arcs 11–17):** Coil's schemes, the Slaughterhouse Nine arrive, the protagonist's rise within the villain world and the cost of it. Stakes: who the protagonist becomes.
4. **Breaking — The wider war (Arcs 18–26):** Open conflict with major powers, the warlord era, the truth about the Protectorate and Cauldron surfacing, Echidna, the shift from local to world stage. Stakes: the future of capes.
5. **End — Gold and gods (Arcs 27–30):** The final revelations, the alliance of everyone against an unstoppable threat, the last desperate plan, and what it costs. Stakes: every world, everyone.

## Factions

Relationship column is *toward the protagonist*, who (per campaign setup) follows Taylor's structural path: a would-be hero whose debut goes sideways and who ends up embedded with the Undersiders.

- **The Undersiders** *(villain team — the protagonist's crew)*: A small, tight team of young villains — heist-focused, loyal to each other above all. Backed by a hidden patron. Archetype: found-family outlaws. **Relationship:** the protagonist's entry point and core relationships; trust is earned and complicated.
- **Empire 88** *(villain — neo-Nazi power bloc)*: The largest cape gang, white-supremacist, led by Kaiser. Many capes, strong turf control. Archetype: organized hate-army. **Relationship:** hostile.
- **Azn Bad Boys (ABB)** *(villain gang)*: Led by **Lung**, a Brute/Changer who escalates without limit in a fight, plus the bomb-tinker **Bakuda**. Smaller but terrifying. **Relationship:** hostile; the protagonist's first major fight is Lung.
- **Archers' Bridge Merchants** *(villain gang)*: Drug-runners and addicts led by Skidmark and Squealer. Disorganized, desperate, dangerous in numbers. **Relationship:** hostile/opportunistic.
- **The Protectorate & the Wards** *(government heroes)*: The official adult heroes (Armsmaster, Miss Militia, Dauntless, Velocity) and their teen branch, the Wards. Backed by the **PRT** (the civilian agency). Archetype: institutional authority. **Relationship:** nominal allies the protagonist can't fully trust; the Wards are peers the protagonist might have joined.
- **The PRT** *(government agency)*: Parahuman Response Teams — non-powered troopers, containment foam, the bureaucracy that manages capes. Archetype: the state. **Relationship:** wary.
- **Coil** *(villain — schemer)*: A calm, mercenary cape running a private army and a long game over the whole city. Pulls strings behind many events. Archetype: spider at the center of the web. **Relationship:** hidden patron-turned-antagonist.
- **The Travelers** *(villain team — outsiders)*: A nomadic team with a tragic secret, drawn into the city's conflicts. Archetype: refugees with a bomb in their luggage.
- **The Slaughterhouse Nine** *(villain — horror)*: A roving band of the worst parahumans alive, led by Jack Slash. They arrive to test and recruit. Archetype: the slasher-movie apocalypse. (Mid-campaign threat.)
- **The Endbringers** *(world threat)*: Leviathan, Behemoth, the Simurgh — monsters that attack cities and trigger forced truces among all capes. Not a faction with goals you can bargain with. Archetype: natural disaster with intent.
- **Cauldron** *(hidden faction)*: A secret organization selling powers and manipulating world events from the shadows. Their purpose is the campaign's deepest reveal. Archetype: the conspiracy that thinks it's the only adult in the room.

## Power → 5e mapping (DM guidance)

This is D&D 5e mechanically, Worm thematically. When the protagonist's power arrives (character file pending), map it to 5e systems rather than inventing whole-cloth:
- A cape's "thing" ≈ a tightly-themed class/subclass + a signature reskinned spell or feature set. Keep the *flavor* fixed (the power doesn't change) even as mechanics scale with level.
- Trigger event ≈ the character's origin/background and the emotional core the DM leans on.
- Classifications above are narrative shorthand for what the build emphasizes (e.g. a Shaker = area-control spells; a Tinker = artificer; a Master = summoner/conjurer; a Thinker = divination/high passive senses).
- NPC capes get stat blocks improvised from the closest SRD monster/NPC plus their signature trick; don't over-engineer — give each one *thing* it does memorably.
- Keep the "unwritten rules" as soft social mechanics: breaking them has faction-reputation consequences, not dice ones.
